Abrahamic religions didn't start dominant—they exploded through political and economic engines that carried them across continents.

- **Christianity's Roman and Colonial Boost**: Emerging in the 1st century CE in the Roman Empire, Christianity benefited from Emperor Constantine's conversion (312 CE), making it the state religion by 380 CE. Later, European colonialism (15th-20th centuries) exported it to the Americas (now ~40% of adherents), sub-Saharan Africa (growing fastest), and Asia via missionaries and trade. Without this imperial scaffolding, Christianity might have stayed a Mediterranean niche faith.
  
- **Islam's Rapid Conquests and Trade Networks**: Founded in the 7th century CE, Islam spread via the Arab-Islamic conquests (632-750 CE), covering the Middle East, North Africa, and into Spain/India. The Silk Road, Indian Ocean trade, and Ottoman/Safavid empires further embedded it in Southeast Asia (e.g., Indonesia, the world's largest Muslim nation) and sub-Saharan Africa. Unlike more insular Eastern traditions, Islam's emphasis on *ummah* (global community) encouraged integration without full cultural erasure.

- **Judaism as the Seed**: As the oldest (~3,500 years), it laid monotheistic foundations but remained small due to diaspora after Roman expulsions (70 CE) and minimal proselytizing. Its influence punches above weight through Christianity and Islam's adoption of Abraham as a patriarch.

In short: Empires did the heavy lifting—Abrahamic faiths "hitched a ride" on Rome, caliphates, and colonial powers, covering ~87% of Earth's land area today (vs. ~13% for Eastern religions like Hinduism/Buddhism).

- **Immigration and Urbanization**: Diaspora communities (e.g., Muslim migrants to Europe, Christian Filipinos abroad) sustain growth. In the U.S. (238 million religious people total), Abrahamics dominate via historical settlement and recent waves.
